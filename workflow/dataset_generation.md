**Step 1 — `athlete_id` is the root key.** IDs are generated sequentially: `athlete_00000`, `athlete_00001`, up to `athlete_99999`. The ID is zero-padded to 5 digits. Everything downstream is seeded from this string, which means the entire generation for a given athlete is deterministic and reproducible.

**Step 2 — OPL row selection (without replacement).** `sample_athlete_persona()` converts the athlete_id string to an integer seed via `int.from_bytes(athlete_id.encode(), "big") % (2**31)`, then calls `random.Random(seed).randint(0, n-1)` to get a candidate row index into `opl_cleaned.csv`. If that index is already taken by another athlete (tracked in `used_indices`), it walks forward one row at a time until it finds a free slot. This guarantees every athlete maps to a different real competition entry. The resolved index is stored as `opl_row_index` in both `sessions.csv` and `block_summary.csv`.

**Step 3 — All persona fields come from that single OPL row.** `sex`, `age`, `bodyweight_kg`, `weight_class_kg`, `squat_peak_kg`, `bench_peak_kg`, `deadlift_peak_kg`, `total_kg`, and `dots` are all read directly from that one row. No interpolation, no generation. The `Dots` score is then passed to `classify_training_level()` which buckets the athlete into novice / intermediate / advanced / elite — this classification drives everything downstream.

**Step 4 — Two independent dataset connections branch from `training_level`.**

The first branch goes back into OPL: `derive_opl_amplitude()` runs across the entire OPL population — not just this athlete's row — to compute the mean inter-meet progression for each training level. So an intermediate athlete's block target is scaled by the typical progression rate observed across thousands of real intermediate competitors. The specific athlete row contributed its Dots score to the level classification, but the amplitude comes from the population.

The second branch goes into the 600K dataset: `precompute_accessory_pools()` builds 16 pre-filtered DataFrames — one per `(training_level, day_index)` combination — by filtering the 600K exercises to the right level and body-part keywords. At generation time, `query_accessories()` just calls `.sample()` on the right pool for this athlete's level and session day. Each sampled `Exercise` object carries `program_title` — the `title` column from `combined_600k_dataset.csv` which is the join key back to `program_summary.csv`.

**Step 5 — `build_training_block()` assembles the 48 sessions.** For each of the 12 weeks × 4 session days, working weight is calculated as `(competition_1rm × (1 + amplitude)) × week_pct[week-1]`, rounded to the nearest 2.5 kg. The `competition_1rm` comes from the OPL row via the persona. The `amplitude` comes from the OPL population. The `week_pct` comes from the literature-derived `PERIODIZATION_SHAPE` scaled by level-specific floor/ceiling. RPE follows the same shape from `RPE_FLOOR_BY_LEVEL` to `RPE_CEILING`.

**Step 6 — Output CSVs store the forward and backward links.** `sessions.csv` gets `opl_row_index` (trace back to OPL) and `accessory_titles` (trace forward to program_summary). `block_summary.csv` gets `opl_row_index`. The seven persona columns — sex, age, bodyweight, squat/bench/deadlift peak, dots — are also written to `sessions.csv` as a composite fingerprint that can recover the correct OPL row even if the index ever drifts.
